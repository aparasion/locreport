This article investigates the interplay between natural language and causal cognition, positing that linguistic expressions both mirror and shape human conceptualizations of causation. The authors begin by surveying foundational philosophical theories regarding causation, highlighting the conflict between metaphysical and judgment-based perspectives. They then explore various linguistic phenomena—such as causative constructions, conditional sentences, and argument structure—illustrating how causal relationships are systematically encoded across different grammatical domains.

Employing insights from linguistics, philosophy, and cognitive science, the research introduces a novel semantic framework for modeling causal knowledge in language. This framework challenges the notion of a uniform correspondence between causal relations and their linguistic expressions, revealing systematic variations in how causality is selected and communicated.

The findings underscore the significance of natural language as a crucial resource for understanding the architecture of causal reasoning and its cognitive representation. This research has implications for fields such as translation studies and communication science, suggesting that linguistic structures can influence cognitive processes related to causation.

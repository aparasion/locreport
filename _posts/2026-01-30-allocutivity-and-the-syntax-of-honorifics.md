This research article explores the intricate relationship between allocutive markers (AMs) and honorifics, focusing on their roles in morphosyntax and discourse. The authors challenge the traditional view that AMs are unembeddable by presenting evidence from various languages where AMs appear in embedded contexts, prompting a reevaluation of how discourse participants are represented in syntactic structures. 

The study delves into the nature of honorifics, which differ from conventional phi-features by being relational and dynamic, reflecting the social relationships among speakers, addressees, and third parties. This raises significant questions about the formal encoding of such discourse-sensitive features. The authors also examine the interaction of AMs and honorifics within both verbal and nominal domains, highlighting theoretical advancements and cross-linguistic insights that have emerged.

The findings underscore the need for a deeper understanding of how morphosyntactic features interact with social dynamics in language. This research has implications for future studies in syntax, discourse analysis, and the broader field of communication science, suggesting new avenues for exploration in the encoding of social relations in language.

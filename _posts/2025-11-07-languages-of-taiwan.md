Taiwan, home to 23.5 million people, showcases a rich linguistic diversity, with Mandarin Chinese as the official language since 1945. While Mandarin is widely spoken and used in public administration and business, Taiwanese Hokkien serves as the primary vernacular language, reflecting the island's historical context. Hakka, spoken by about 6% of the population, and various indigenous languages, collectively known as Formosan languages, also contribute to Taiwan's linguistic landscape, although they face declining usage. English is increasingly important due to Taiwan's status as a tech hub, with a growing number of younger speakers. The article highlights the impact of historical governance on language policies and the ongoing efforts by the Taiwanese government to preserve local languages. As Taiwan continues to evolve, the prominence of English and other Asian languages is expected to rise, while indigenous languages may struggle to survive.

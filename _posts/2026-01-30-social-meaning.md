This review article examines two interrelated research programs in sociolinguistics focused on social meaning: "social meaning as reasoning" and "social meaning as a link between language and power." The first program investigates the cognitive processes involved in interpreting sociolinguistic variants, while the second explores how language contributes to power dynamics and the maintenance of social structures. The author argues that a comprehensive understanding of social meaning requires an integration of these perspectives, positing that insights into social relations enhance our grasp of cognitive reasoning in language use, and vice versa.

The review proposes a novel model that synthesizes these approaches, drawing inspiration from game-theoretic pragmatics. This framework aims to illuminate the complex interplay between language, cognition, and social inequality, suggesting that the study of social meaning cannot be fully realized without considering both cognitive and sociopolitical dimensions.

The findings have significant implications for language theory and research, particularly in understanding how linguistic choices reflect and perpetuate social hierarchies, thus contributing to ongoing discussions in language technology, translation studies, and communication science.

On November 27, 2025, a significant event unfolded as students across Europe engaged in a translation contest that emphasized traditional skills over modern technology. Participants were tasked with translating texts into any two of the 24 official EU languages, but they were strictly prohibited from using machine translation (MT), AI tools, or computer-aided translation (CAT) software. This initiative, organized by the European Commission, involved 3,004 students from 716 schools, highlighting the ongoing commitment to fostering linguistic diversity and traditional translation practices in an increasingly digital world.

This contest reflects a broader trend within the localization industry where there is a growing debate about the role of technology in translation. As machine translation and AI-driven tools become more prevalent, concerns about the erosion of linguistic skills and the quality of translations have emerged. The European Commission's decision to exclude these technologies from the contest serves as a reminder of the importance of human translators and the nuanced understanding they bring to the craft. This moment is particularly relevant as localization managers and language technology leaders navigate the balance between leveraging advanced tools and maintaining the integrity of human translation.

The implications for localization workflows are profound. By emphasizing human translation skills, this contest may influence how educational institutions approach language training, potentially leading to a new generation of translators who prioritize creativity and linguistic fluency over reliance on technology. For localization teams, this could mean a shift in hiring practices, with an increased focus on candidates who demonstrate strong traditional translation capabilities. Additionally, vendors may need to adapt their offerings to support a hybrid model that values both human expertise and technological efficiency, ensuring that they remain competitive in a market that prizes quality alongside speed.

This event signals a critical juncture for the localization industry. As the demand for high-quality translations continues to grow, the emphasis on human skill in translation contests like this one highlights the need for a balanced approach. The LocReport editorial team observes that while technology will undoubtedly play a crucial role in the future of localization, the enduring value of human translators cannot be overlooked. The challenge for the industry will be to integrate advanced tools while nurturing the essential human elements of creativity and cultural understanding that define successful translation. This contest serves as a reminder that, in the quest for efficiency, the art of translation must not be sacrificed.

Nearly 69 million people in the United States speak a language other than English at home, yet weather warnings have long been issued almost exclusively in English. A new study published in the journal *Artificial Intelligence for the Earth Systems* documents how the National Weather Service is using artificial intelligence to change that, developing a comprehensive translation program that delivers life-saving forecasts and alerts in Spanish, Chinese, Vietnamese, Samoan, French and other languages.

The study was led by University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign climate, meteorology and atmospheric sciences professor Joseph Trujillo-Falcón and NWS scientist Monica Bozeman.

## The stakes

The urgency for accurate and culturally sensitive translation of weather forecasts in the US has deep historical roots. In 1987, an F4 tornado struck Saragosa, Texas, injuring or killing 151 of the town's 183 residents. The town had many Spanish-only speakers and only one Spanish-language radio station — which did broadcast the NWS warning, but the literal English-to-Spanish translation failed because the word "warning" has no direct Spanish equivalent.

That failure of terminology, not technology, shaped how researchers now think about weather translation: it is not a word-substitution problem but a domain-specific language problem that requires trained models and cultural adaptation.

## The AI program

The NWS has partnered with AI translation platform LILT, whose patented training process enables large language models to adapt neural machine translation tools for weather-specific terminology and messaging.

The operational results are significant. Before the program, bilingual NWS forecasters spent up to an hour manually translating a single product. The AI system now completes the same translation in 5 to 7 minutes for some NWS products, with accuracy scores above 95%. More than 30 NWS offices across the country are currently using it.

Starting with the 2025 hurricane season, the National Hurricane Center officially began issuing AI-translated Spanish advisories for all hurricanes in the Pacific and Atlantic coasts — providing life-saving information not only to US residents but also to Latin America.

## Multilingual coverage by geography

The program's scope extends beyond a single language pair. Illinois geography and GIS graduate student Liam Llewellyn has overlaid geographic information systems, weather and census data to identify which language translations are needed for all 122 NWS offices across the country — a methodology that treats translation coverage as a geographic resource allocation problem.

With the 2026 World Cup approaching, the NWS has also made an agency-wide effort to provide decision-support services in the event of dangerous storms approaching stadiums, extending the program's reach to international visitors.

## Relevance for the language services industry

The NWS case illustrates two dynamics of current relevance to the language services sector:

**Domain adaptation at speed.** The accuracy threshold achieved — above 95% on domain-specific weather content — reflects a training methodology tuned for a controlled, high-stakes vocabulary rather than general-purpose translation. That specialisation is precisely what the [multilingual LLM gap](/intelligence/signals/multilingual-llm-gap/) signal tracks: the divergence between general-purpose model performance and domain-adapted model performance across language pairs.

**Institutional adoption in critical sectors.** Government and public-safety organisations have historically been among the most cautious adopters of AI translation, due to liability concerns and the consequences of error. The NWS program, now operational across more than 30 offices and formalised at the National Hurricane Center, marks a meaningful threshold for institutional confidence in AI translation quality.

The research team is also pursuing social science evaluation of how the public responds to AI-translated tools, in collaboration with Spanish and Portuguese professor Salvatore Callesano at Illinois. That work — assessing not just accuracy but comprehension and trust — is the kind of downstream evaluation that will matter as AI translation moves into additional public-sector contexts.

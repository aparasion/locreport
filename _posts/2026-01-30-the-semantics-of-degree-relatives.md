This article explores the semantics of degree relatives within relative clauses, addressing a gap in the uniform study of their diverse constructions. The author argues that degree relatives should be understood as relative clauses that can quantify over degrees, rather than just individuals, thereby providing a cohesive framework that integrates existing theories of relativization and degree semantics.

The methodology involves a comprehensive review of various constructions, such as equatives, amount relatives, and wh-exclamatives, and synthesizes insights from degree semantics to elucidate their behavior. The findings suggest that degree readings are accessible when specific morphological cues, contextual dimensions of measurement, and determinate individual referents are present. This approach clarifies previously puzzling aspects of degree relatives and demonstrates how they can be systematically analyzed.

The theoretical implications of this work are significant, as they unify disparate proposals under a single semantic framework. Practically, this research can inform computational linguistics and translation studies by enhancing the understanding of relative clauses and their nuanced meanings in different contexts.

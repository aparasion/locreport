The Latin script, now the world's most widely used writing system, influences not only communication but also cultural identity and power dynamics. With approximately 4.7 to 4.9 billion users across over 150 languages, its reach is vast, yet its adoption has often been fraught with historical complexities, including colonialism and cultural negotiation. This article delves into how writing systems, particularly the Latin alphabet, shape perceptions of language and identity, highlighting case studies from Malta, Vietnam, Türkiye, and Kazakhstan.

For localization and language services professionals, understanding the implications of script choice is crucial. The transition from one writing system to another can redefine how a culture archives its history and interacts with the global market. The modifications made to the Latin script—such as diacritics and digraphs—reflect ongoing negotiations between tradition and modernity.

One key takeaway is that script reform is not merely an aesthetic decision; it is a significant cultural shift that can impact localization strategies, requiring professionals to adapt their approaches to align with evolving linguistic landscapes.

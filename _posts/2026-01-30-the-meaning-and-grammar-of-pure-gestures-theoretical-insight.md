This study explores the role of "pure gestures," which are gestures produced independently from speech, revealing significant insights into their linguistic properties. The research identifies three key findings: first, the iconic content of pure gestures can be dynamically allocated among traditional inferential language slots, suggesting that language structure may arise more from productive processes than from fixed lexicons. Second, the grammar of certain pure gestures exhibits similarities to constructions found in sign languages, raising questions about the influence of Universal Grammar versus deeper cognitive mechanisms. Lastly, the study posits that pure gestures possess a unique iconic syntax akin to that of sign language classifiers. These findings underscore the relevance of gestures in linguistic theory and advocate for a multimodal approach that integrates gestures and signs, enriching our understanding of communication and language structure.

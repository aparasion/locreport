There is a fiction that still circulates in parts of the language services industry: that AI companies are technology vendors, and language services companies are the real language businesses. The fiction is becoming harder to maintain.

[OpenAI](https://openai.com), [Anthropic](https://anthropic.com), [Google DeepMind](https://deepmind.google), [Meta AI](https://ai.meta.com), [Mistral](https://mistral.ai), and [Cohere](https://cohere.com) are not incidentally in the language business. They are, in the most fundamental sense, language companies. Their products are built from human language, trained on human language, and evaluated by whether they can produce human language that meets the standards of accuracy, fluency, and cultural coherence that professional translators have spent decades defining. The fact that they call themselves AI companies does not change the substance of what they do.

When Meta releases [NLLB (No Language Left Behind)](https://ai.meta.com/research/no-language-left-behind/) — a multilingual model trained on 200 languages — that is a language strategy decision with direct consequences for the localization industry. When Anthropic decides how much multilingual evaluation data to include in Claude's training, that is a product decision that determines which markets get near-human quality output and which do not. When Google's translation team publishes a paper at [ACL 2026](/events/#event-card-acl-2026) on low-resource language performance, that paper will be in production pipelines within 18 months.

We have been tracking these companies obliquely for some time — through their influence on machine translation quality, through the [signal tracker](/intelligence/signals/), through [monthly reports](/reports/monthly/) that reference LLM capabilities. We are formalizing that coverage now.

## What is changing

**LocStock now includes an AI Platforms segment.** We have added five companies to [LocStock](/market/) — [NVIDIA](https://investor.nvidia.com) (NVDA), [Meta Platforms](https://investor.fb.com) (META), [Amazon](https://ir.aboutamazon.com) (AMZN), [SoundHound AI](https://ir.soundhound.com) (SOUN), and [C3.ai](https://ir.c3.ai) (AI) — alongside [IBM](https://www.ibm.com/investor) (IBM) in the Enterprise Software segment. These are publicly traded companies whose infrastructure decisions, model releases, and financial performance are directly relevant to the language services market. NVIDIA's GPU allocation shapes which AI labs can train at what scale. Meta's NLLB initiative is the most ambitious multilingual model effort by any technology company. Amazon Translate remains one of the highest-volume MT engines in production enterprise use. The [LocStock AI segment](/market/) tracks them alongside traditional LSPs, Big Tech, and BPO providers in a single view.

**Our events calendar now covers four AI and NLP conferences.** The [Association for Computational Linguistics](https://aclweb.org) annual meeting ([ACL 2026](/events/#event-card-acl-2026)) and [EMNLP 2026](/events/#event-card-emnlp-2026) have always been the venues where the research driving machine translation advances is first published. [NeurIPS 2026](/events/#event-card-neurips-2026) is where foundational model architecture decisions are announced. [World Summit AI Amsterdam](/events/#event-card-world-summit-ai-amsterdam) brings together enterprise AI buyers and policymakers whose decisions shape deployment at scale. We are adding all four to the calendar alongside [LocWorld](/events/locworld/), [SlatorCon](/events/slatorcon/), ATA, and EAMT. The editorial filter applies: we cover what matters for language, translation, and the people who work in it.

**Two new signals are now active.** The [multilingual LLM gap](/intelligence/signals/multilingual-llm-gap/) signal tracks the growing body of evidence that AI translation quality is not uniform across languages — that the gap between high-resource and low-resource language pair performance is widening as models scale, creating a two-tier market with different implications for buyers, LSPs, and translators depending on which languages they work with. The [AI company language strategy](/intelligence/signals/ai-company-language-strategy/) signal tracks how AI labs are making — or failing to make — explicit product decisions about multilingual coverage. Both signals are now live in the [intelligence dashboard](/intelligence/).

## What is not changing

LocReport's editorial filter is unchanged: does this matter for language, translation, and the professionals who work in both? AI chip supply chains, image generation, and enterprise SaaS metrics are not our beat. Multilingual model evaluations, low-resource language research, AI workforce displacement in the translation sector, and the strategic choices AI labs make about which languages to invest in — those are.

The language services industry has spent the last several years watching AI companies from a cautious distance, tracking their output quality on WMT benchmarks and FLORES leaderboards while treating the companies themselves as external forces. That posture made sense when MT was a commodity layer. It makes less sense when the companies building the models are also making the training data decisions, the language coverage decisions, and the evaluation standard decisions that determine what professional translation is competing against.

We cover the language industry. These are now language industry companies.
